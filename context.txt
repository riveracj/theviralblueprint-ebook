CHAPTER 2
Finding Your Sound
Developing your style, voice, identity, and brand
Before you can build fans, you need to know who you are as an artist.
Your sound is your fingerprint — it's what makes someone hear 5
seconds of your music and know immediately that it's you.
This sounds simple, but most new artists skip this step entirely. They start recording random songs in
random styles, never committing to a lane, and end up with a confusing catalog that doesn't attract a
consistent audience. This chapter will help you figure out exactly who you are as an artist before you
release a single song.
The 4 Pillars of Your Sound
Your artistic identity is built from four key elements. You don't need to have all four perfected before
you start — but you need to be actively developing each one:
1 Your Flow
How you ride a beat — your rhythm, pacing, cadence. Do you rap fast or slow? Do you ride on top of the
beat or behind it? Fast flows (Eminem, Tech N9ne) vs melodic flows (Future, Rod Wave) vs conversational
flows (J. Cole, Kendrick).
2 Your Voice Tone
The natural quality of your voice. Deep and authoritative? High and melodic? Rough and gritty? You can't
change your voice — but you can learn to use it as your greatest weapon.

3 Your Topics
What do you write about? Pain and struggle? Success and ambition? Humor and storytelling? Love and
relationships? The most successful artists have a clear theme running through their work that their fans
connect with.
4 Your Visual Brand
How you look and present yourself matters just as much as the music. Your photos, your videos, your
clothing, your color palette — these all communicate who you are before anyone hits play.

Here is a practical rule that will accelerate your development faster than any course or coaching:
record 10 songs before you judge yourself. Not 2. Not 5. Ten. The point of your first 10 songs is not to

Building Your Artist Brand
Your brand is everything outside of the music itself — your name, your look, your aesthetic, your
social media presence. All of it communicates something about you before a single note plays. Here's
how to build a brand that attracts fans:
Choosing Your Artist Name
Your artist name should be: easy to spell, easy to say, unique enough to Google without finding 50
other people, and meaningful to you. Check that the Instagram handle, Twitter handle, and YouTube

Your Visual Aesthetic
Pick 2-3 colors that represent your brand and use them consistently on everything — your cover art,
your social media posts, your merch. Think about what clothing style fits your music. Drake wears
luxury streetwear because it matches his aspirational lifestyle content. Kendrick wears understated,
thoughtful clothing because it matches his intellectual brand. What does YOUR music call for?

r/makinghiphop, r/hiphopheads, or r/rapbattles. Join Discord servers for music producers and ask for
honest critiques. Consider hiring a professional A&R; consultant or music coach for one session. Real

CHAPTER 4
Getting a Record Deal
How to get signed the right way and avoid getting scammed
A record deal can be a rocket ship that launches your career to the
stratosphere — or it can be a cage that traps you for years while someone
else profits from your work. The music industry is full of contracts
designed to take advantage of artists who don't know what they're
signing. This chapter will make sure you're not one of them.
Do You Even Need a Record Deal?
Before you chase a deal, ask yourself a serious question: what does a label actually give you that you
can't get yourself? In the 1990s, labels were essential because they controlled everything —
distribution, radio, marketing, and physical retail. Today, you can do all of that yourself. Here's what
labels provide versus what you can do independently:
What a Label Provides Can You Do It Yourself?
Music distribution to stores YES — DistroKid for $22/year
Marketing budget YES — organic social media is free and often more effective
Radio promotion Increasingly irrelevant for rap — streaming matters more
Tour support and booking YES — book your own local shows starting out
Studio time YES — home recording is professional quality now
Advance (cash upfront) YES — crowdfunding, side income, or a day job
International reach Streaming is already global with no label needed
Credibility and cosign Build it yourself — often more respected now
The honest conclusion: labels make sense when you need massive tour support, major radio push, or
international marketing at scale. For most independent artists just starting out, going independent and
keeping 100% of your rights is the smarter move. Chance the Rapper built a Grammy-winning career
with zero label and kept every dollar.

The Modern Path to Getting Signed
Labels don't sign potential anymore — they sign proof. The days of a demo tape getting you a deal
are mostly over. Today, labels want to see that you already have an audience before they invest in
you. Here's the path that actually works:
1 Build numbers first
Get to at least 50,000 monthly Spotify listeners OR 25,000+ TikTok followers before approaching any
label. Labels want to invest in something already growing. Show them proof, not potential.
2 Target the right type of deal
Start with independent labels (Empire, AWAL, Rostrum, Def Jam Recordings) before going to majors.
They're more accessible, move faster, and often offer better deal terms.
3 Find A&R contacts
Every major and indie label has A&R reps whose job is to find new talent. Find them on LinkedIn and
Instagram. Build a relationship before pitching. A cold DM with a link to your music almost never works —
a warm relationship does.
4 Submit professionally
Your pitch should include: a 2-paragraph bio, your best 2-3 songs, your streaming stats, your social media
numbers, and what makes you unique. Keep it short and factual.
5 ALWAYS get a music attorney
Before you sign ANYTHING — a management contract, a label deal, a publishing deal, a producer
agreement — hire a music attorney. This is non-negotiable. A good music attorney costs $200-500/hour
and will save you from losing millions.

CHAPTER 5
Making Money as an Artist
Every income stream working rappers use to build real wealth
Here's the truth most people don't know: streaming royalties are the
smallest piece of the money pie for most successful rappers. The artists
who build real wealth understand that music is a business with multiple
income streams — and they work ALL of them simultaneously.
The 8 Income Streams of a Successful Rapper
1. Streaming Royalties — Your Passive Foundation
Spotify pays approximately $0.003-0.005 per stream. Apple Music pays slightly more at ~$0.007. A
song with 1 million streams earns roughly $3,000-5,000. That sounds small, but consider: a catalog of
30 songs each getting 100,000 streams monthly is $9,000-15,000 per month passively. The key is
building a catalog over time, not chasing one viral hit. Register your music with a PRO (Performing
Rights Organization) — ASCAP or BMI in the US — to collect ALL royalties including performance
royalties when your music plays on radio or TV.
2. Live Performances — Where the Real Money Lives
For most working rappers, live shows are their primary income. Starting out: $200-500 per local open
mic or club show. With 5,000-10,000 monthly Spotify listeners: $500-2,000 per show. With 50,000+
monthly listeners: $2,000-15,000 per show. With 500,000+ monthly listeners: $15,000-100,000+ per
show. The math: even a mid-level artist doing 2 shows a week at $2,000 each earns $208,000 a year
from shows alone. Build your live show first in your city, then expand to regional tours, then national.
3. Merchandise — High Margin, Low Overhead

Merchandise has some of the highest profit margins of any business. A hoodie that costs $12 to
produce can sell for $55-80 at a show or online. Start with print-on-demand: Printful, Printify, or Merch
by Amazon have zero upfront cost. You design it, they print and ship when someone orders. As you
grow, move to bulk orders for higher profit margins. Artists with 50,000 followers can easily earn
$5,000-15,000/month in merchandise.
4. Brand Deals and Sponsorships
Once you have an engaged audience — even a small one — brands will pay to reach them. At
5,000-10,000 followers with strong engagement: $200-1,000 per sponsored post. At 50,000-100,000
followers: $1,000-10,000 per deal. At 500,000+ followers: $10,000-500,000+ per campaign. The key
is engagement rate, not just follower count. An artist with 10,000 real fans who engage with every
post is more valuable to a brand than one with 100,000 ghost followers.
5. Sync Licensing — The Hidden Goldmine
Sync licensing means your music gets placed in TV shows, movies, commercials, video games, and
YouTube videos in exchange for a fee. This is one of the most overlooked income streams. A single
TV placement on a show like Power or Empire can earn $5,000-50,000. A major commercial
placement can earn $50,000-500,000+. Register with ASCAP or BMI and a sync licensing agency
like Musicbed, Artlist, or Musicbed to start getting your music placed in productions.
6. YouTube Ad Revenue
YouTube pays creators through Google AdSense based on ad views on their videos. Average
earnings are $1-5 per 1,000 views (CPM varies by audience). A channel with 500,000 subscribers
posting 2 videos per week can earn $5,000-15,000/month in ad revenue alone — completely
separate from music streaming. This is a long game, but artists who commit to YouTube consistently
build a genuinely passive income stream.
7. Songwriting and Producing for Other Artists
You don't have to be the one performing the song to make money from it. Songwriters earn royalties
every time a song they wrote gets played — forever. If you can write hits, you can earn publishing
royalties from other artists' songs that you contributed to. Producers earn a front-end fee
($500-$50,000+ per beat) plus backend royalty points (2-4% of album royalties).

CHAPTER 6
The Money Behind the Music
Making serious money in rap without ever touching a mic
Not everyone is meant to be on the mic — and that is completely fine.
Some of the wealthiest people in the rap industry have never rapped a
single bar. For every artist making $50,000 a show, there is a team of
people each earning their cut: a manager, a booking agent, a producer, a
videographer, a publicist, an attorney. The music industry is a machine —
and every part of that machine needs skilled operators.
9 Ways to Build Wealth in Rap Without Being the Artist
1. Beat Producer / Music Producer
Producers create the instrumental beats that rappers perform over. This is one of the most lucrative
behind-the-scenes roles in music. Top producers like Metro Boomin, Murda Beatz, and Southside
have earned tens of millions from beat sales, production royalties, and credits. Starting out, you can
sell beats on BeatStars for $20-5,000 each. Producers typically earn a front-end fee when the beat is
sold PLUS 2-4% royalty points on all album sales and streams — meaning you get paid every time
that song earns money, forever. Learn production free on GarageBand or invest $200 in FL Studio.
Watch YouTube tutorials daily. Build a catalog of 50+ beats before reaching out to artists.
2. Artist Manager
A manager is the CEO of the artist's business. They book shows, negotiate deals, handle day-to-day
decisions, and build the artist's long-term career strategy. Managers typically earn 15-20% of
everything the artist makes — music, shows, deals, merch. Sign one artist doing $300,000 a year and
you are making $45,000-60,000 from that one client. The best managers don't just manage talent —
they develop it. They see the vision before the artist's numbers justify it and build the infrastructure for

success. You can start by managing a talented local artist for free or a small percentage, learn the
business through doing, and grow from there. No degree or certification is required — results are
your resume.
3. Music Videographer and Editor
Every rapper needs music videos, social media content, behind-the-scenes footage, and promotional
clips — and most of them need this content constantly. A skilled music videographer charges
$500-15,000 per music video depending on production quality. Short-form content creators who
specialize in TikTok and Instagram Reels can charge $200-2,000 per batch of content. You do not
need expensive equipment to start. Many of the most viral music videos were shot on an iPhone with
good lighting and creative direction. Learn editing on free tools like CapCut, DaVinci Resolve, or
iMovie, then upgrade to Premiere Pro as you earn more. Build your portfolio by shooting 5-10 local
artists for free or cheap, then use those to charge professional rates.
4. Mixing and Mastering Engineer
Every song you hear on a professional release — whether it's on Spotify, the radio, or in a movie —
has been mixed and mastered by a professional audio engineer. Mixing is the process of blending all
the individual tracks (vocals, beats, effects) into a balanced, cohesive sound. Mastering is the final
polish that makes it loud and consistent across all playback devices. Mixing engineers charge
$100-2,000 per song. Mastering engineers charge $50-500 per song. With a consistent client load of
5 songs per week at $200 per mix, you are earning $4,000/month from a home studio with a laptop.
Learn on free software (Audacity, GarageBand) then invest in a proper DAW. Take online courses on
Coursera, Udemy, or YouTube — many working engineers are entirely self-taught.
5. Music Publicist
A publicist's job is to generate press coverage and media attention for an artist. They pitch stories to
hip-hop blogs, magazines, podcasts, and TV shows. They write press releases, coordinate
interviews, and manage the artist's public narrative. Music publicists work on monthly retainers
ranging from $1,000-10,000 per client. With 5 clients at $2,500/month, you are earning
$150,000/year. Build relationships with hip-hop media outlets before you have clients — follow
editors on social media, engage with their content, send story pitches. Then offer your services to
local artists and use the press you generate as your portfolio.

6. Booking Agent
Booking agents connect artists with venues, festivals, and promoters for live performances. They
earn 10-15% of every show fee as their commission. Book an artist for a $10,000 show and you
pocket $1,000-1,500 for one negotiation. Top booking agents at agencies like WME, CAA, and
